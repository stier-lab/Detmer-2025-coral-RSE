‚¨ÜÔ∏è Growth

Corals can grow to the next larger size class (e.g., SC2 ‚Üí SC3). Can't skip classes ‚Äî no jumping from SC1 to SC3.

‚¨áÔ∏è Shrinkage

Partial mortality from disease, predation, or stress. Corals can shrink to any smaller class (e.g., SC4 ‚Üí SC1 in severe cases).

‚è∏Ô∏è Stasis

Stay in the same size class. Most common outcome ‚Äî not all corals grow every year. Probability = 1 - (growth + shrinkage).

üíî Fragmentation

Only SC4 and SC5 break apart (e.g., during storms), creating multiple smaller colonies. This is asexual reproduction ‚Äî increases total population.

üê£ Reproduction

SC3+ produce larvae. Larger corals produce exponentially more (SC5 might produce 100,000+ larvae/year).

‚ò†Ô∏è Mortality

Each size class has a survival probability (typically 40‚Äì90%). Smaller corals die more easily.

Core Population Equation

N(t+1) = S ¬∑ (T + F) ¬∑ N(t) + R
N(t)
Population Vector
Number of corals in each size class at time t
S
Survival Matrix
Diagonal matrix with size-specific survival probabilities
T
Transition Matrix
Growth, shrinkage, and stasis probabilities
F
Fragmentation Matrix
Asexual reproduction via colony breakage
R
Recruitment Vector
New larvae settling from external and lab sources
üî¨ Ecological Interpretation
  1. Start with your population in year t
  2. Some corals die (multiply by S)
  3. Survivors grow, shrink, or stay same size (multiply by T)
  4. Large corals fragment, creating new individuals (add F)
  5. New larvae settle, adding recruits (add R)
  6. Result = population in year t+1
S - Survival
Diagonal
0.4 ‚Äì 0.9

Each size class has its own survival rate. SC1: 40%, SC5: 90%. Small changes here dramatically affect population growth (Œª).

T - Transition
5√ó5 Matrix
Œ£ = 1.0

Each column sums to 1. Above diagonal = growth, below = shrinkage, diagonal = stasis. Encodes all size class movement probabilities.

F - Fragmentation
5√ó5 Matrix
Œ£ > 1.0

Columns can exceed 1 because fragmentation creates new individuals! Only SC4 and SC5 produce fragments.

R - Recruitment
Vector
‚Üí SC1

New settlers almost always enter SC1 (smallest class). Sources: wild larvae + lab outplants from all treatments.

Annual Model Cycle

‚è±Ô∏è Sequence Matters
These 9 steps execute in order every model year. Understanding the sequence helps you see where bottlenecks occur and when constraints apply.
1
Survival & Growth (Reef)
Existing corals on the reef either die, grow, shrink, or stay the same size. Large corals (SC4, SC5) can fragment. External recruits (wild larvae) settle into SC1.
N_reef(t+1) = S ¬∑ (T + F) ¬∑ N_reef(t) + external_recruits
2
Survival & Growth (Orchard)
Same process in the nursery orchard, but with higher survival and faster growth (protected environment). No fragmentation in the orchard.
N_orchard(t+1) = S ¬∑ T ¬∑ N_orchard(t)
3
Reproduction
Corals in SC3+ produce larvae. Calculate total larvae from orchard, reference reefs, and restoration reef.
larvae = Œ£(N[i] √ó fecundity[i]) for i ‚àà {SC3, SC4, SC5}
4
Larvae Collection
Collect fraction of larvae from orchard (orchard_yield) and reference reefs (reef_yield). Total larvae capped at lab capacity (lab_max).
5
Lab Settlement & Survival
Larvae settle on tiles with different success rates. Split into immediate (0_TX) and 1-year retention (1_TX) treatments. Density dependence applies.
survival = s √ó exp(-m √ó density)
6
Outplanting Allocation
Surviving settlers distributed to reef and orchard based on reef_prop parameter. Further distributed across multiple reefs/orchards if configured.
7
Space Constraints
Check if destinations have space. Reef: if occupied ‚â• reef_areas, block outplants. Orchard: if colonies ‚â• orchard_size, calculate partial acceptance.
8
Add Outplants
Successfully outplanted corals added to destinations, distributed across size classes according to size_props (e.g., 70% SC1, 30% SC2 if they've grown in lab).
9
Transplanting (Optional)
If scheduled, move large orchard colonies directly to reef. Useful for rapid coral cover increase and boosting reproduction.
üîÑ The Feedback Loop
Orchard ‚Üí Larvae ‚Üí Lab ‚Üí Orchard ‚Üí Repeat
This positive feedback loop means initial investment in an orchard pays off exponentially as orchard corals mature and produce more larvae for the lab. This is why orchard_yield is such a critical parameter!

Most Important Parameters

üéØ Parameter Sensitivity
Not all parameters matter equally! Survival (especially SC1-3) and lab capacity have the biggest impact on restoration success. A 10% increase in SC2 survival has more impact than doubling fragmentation rates.
üß¨ Survival
CRITICAL
0.4 ‚Äì 0.9

Fraction surviving each year, by size class. Most influential parameter. Small changes (0.7 ‚Üí 0.75) can shift from population decline to growth. Focus on keeping young corals alive!

üî¨ Lab Capacity
BOTTLENECK
10k ‚Äì 40k

Maximum larvae processed per year. Common bottleneck. If orchard produces 100k larvae but lab_max = 20k, you lose 80k potential settlers. Expanding lab capacity often has high ROI.

üå± Orchard Yield
FEEDBACK
0.0 ‚Äì 1.0

Fraction of orchard larvae collected. Determines feedback loop strength. Higher yield = more sustainable restoration with less dependence on unpredictable wild spawning. Investment in collection infrastructure pays off over time.

ü™∏ Reef Allocation
TRADE-OFF
0.0 ‚Äì 1.0

Fraction of outplants to reef vs. orchard. Key strategic decision: Immediate reef restoration vs. building orchard "seed bank" for long-term sustainability. Optimal balance depends on your timeline and goals.

‚¨ÜÔ∏è Growth Rate
TIMING
0.0 ‚Äì 0.3

Probability of growing to next size class. Faster growth = quicker path to reproduction. Getting corals to SC3+ (reproductive size) is critical because that's when they start contributing larvae.

üíî Fragmentation
ASEXUAL
0.0 ‚Äì 0.3

Rate of asexual reproduction via colony breakage (SC4-5 only). Can double population growth in high-energy environments. One SC5 colony might produce 0.6 new corals via fragmentation in a single storm season.

üê£ Fecundity
SCALING
0 ‚Äì 100k

Larvae produced per coral per year. Scales exponentially with size: SC1-2 don't reproduce, SC5 produces 100,000+ larvae. Large adults are larval "factories" ‚Äî protecting them is crucial for population growth.

üìç Reef Capacity
CONSTRAINT
10‚Åµ ‚Äì 10‚Å∑ cm¬≤

Total space available on restoration reef. Hard limit: once full, no more outplants accepted. Choose restoration sites with adequate space. 80,000 cm¬≤ = 8 m¬≤ (small patch), 10 million cm¬≤ = 1,000 m¬≤ (large reef area).

üåø Settlement Rate
EFFICIENCY
0.8 ‚Äì 0.95

Fraction of larvae successfully settling on tiles. Small differences compound: 95% vs. 90% means 5% more settlers from the same larvae. Tile type (cement vs. ceramic) affects this rate.

‚è±Ô∏è Lab Survival
TREATMENT
0.6 ‚Äì 0.95

Fraction surviving to outplanting. Immediate (s0 ‚âà 0.9) vs. 1-year retention (s1 ‚âà 0.7). Trade-off: immediate has higher survival but smaller size at outplanting. 1-year produces larger outplants but more die in lab.

üìä Density Dependence
CROWDING
0.0 ‚Äì 0.05

How crowding reduces survival: survival = s √ó exp(-m √ó N). m=0: no effect, m=0.05: strong crowding. Cramming 10,000 larvae in limited space causes survival to drop. This creates quality vs. quantity trade-off.

üèóÔ∏è Orchard Size
INVESTMENT
100 ‚Äì 10k

Maximum colonies orchard can hold (all sizes combined). Larger orchards produce more larvae but cost more to maintain. 200 = small (30 trees √ó 7 corals/tree), 2,400 = large (80 trees √ó 30 corals/tree). ROI improves as orchard matures.
